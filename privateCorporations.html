Private Corporations

Me: Yea, what is all this controversy about Google and Facebook and privacy? What's the worst thing private companies can do? Provide personalized online ads? That doesn't seem problematic at all.


Privacy Advocate: Great question. Countless private companies are in the data collection business and there are countless ways in which they violate your privacy. For example, email providers (Gmail) who can read your emails, instant messaging services that can read your messages (Facebook Messenger and Snapchat), internet service providers that can record every single website you visit, cloud storage providers that can read through your files (Dropbox), navigation apps that can record your location at all times (Google Maps), video platforms that record every video you watch (YouTube), social media platforms that record your every post, comment, like and page view and search engines that record everything you've ever searched on internet. I cannot provide you an exhaustive list of violations here, but I would encourage you to look at the Recommended App List (included in the upcoming digital privacy guide) to see which providers you should use or avoid. Remember, personal data is extremely valuable and many app makers have large incentives to collect every bit of data they can get from you.

Again, if you feel a little fearful or anxious when you read this, relax. Such feelings are counterproductive. We will get onto some practical solutions to address these issues.


Me: Ok. I understand many of these private companies want my data and they sometimes violate my privacy to get it. But, what's the harm? What's the worst they can do? Try to sell me stuff?


Privacy Advocate: Well, as we discussed before, the government certainly can do harmful things with personal data and these private companies often provide such data to the government. Your data can be used against you in many other ways as well.

Here are a few examples:

  1. In 2019, Kyle Kashuv had his admission to Harvard rescinded after his political opponents published private chats, from Google and Skype, in which he used racial slurs.

  2. In 2017, Kyle Quinn, a university professor, was mistaken for a white supremacist protestor on social media. His opponents used whatever personal data they could get to threaten his life, damage his reputation and remove him from his job. Having certain personal data online can be a significant liability.

  3. Supporters of Proposition 8, in California in 2008, were sometimes boycotted, harassed or fired because of their donations. Their data/identities were revealed, and some suffered negative consequences. (Regardless of your political leanings, recognize that supporting any political issue can be riskier if your opponents can get their hands on your personal data).

  4. Starting in 2009, China has been implementing a “Social Scoring” system which, using massive personal data collection from several private companies, assigns a score to each citizen. Citizens who exhibit “bad” behavior, such as arguing with a neighbor, jaywalking or hanging with the “wrong crowd”, are penalized. Punishments include slower internet speeds, restricted use of public transit, and prohibitions on buying luxury items or getting a mortgage.

  5. A Canadian lawsuit, from 2014, may have set a precedent that would allow insurance companies to use data, from personal activity trackers, to reject insurance claims that are not verified by such data. Data from these trackers are very detailed and particularly personal. With the widespread use of these devices, more and more private elements of the user's life will be recorded.


Me: Wow, I do agree those examples are troubling. This makes me wonder how much of my private data is being collected, without my knowledge. And who knows what it's being used for? I use so many apps. I'm sure a lot of my data is all over the internet or in some databases. Honestly, I feel discouraged just thinking about it. There is some data that I don't want to share, but I'm not sure how I can get control of this stuff.


Privacy Advocate: As I've said before, don't worry too much. Let's focus on what you can control. Anxiety is not helpful. Remember, this is your life and your data. Ultimately, you will need to decide what level of privacy you're comfortable with. You need to examine the apps you use, and the companies that produce them, and decide which ones you trust and which you don't. This is your choice, not mine. I simply hope that this guide will be useful in your digital privacy journey.
