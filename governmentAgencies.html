Government Agencies

Me: I'm fine with government collecting my data. I'm sure they're using the data for good purposes like protecting innocent people or fighting our enemies abroad. Why is this a problem? I have nothing to hide, therefore, I have nothing to fear. I'm not doing anything illegal so I'm not worried. If I have something I don't want anyone to know, maybe I shouldn't be doing it in the first place.



Privacy Advocate: Remember the strange man at the airport? If you have nothing to hide, why did you hide your smartphone from him? Why didn't you let him continue to watch you?



Me: Well he could be a malicious hacker. I need to keep my personal data safe.



Privacy Advocate: What if that man at the airport was a government agent? Would you behave any differently? Would you have any problem with this glorified government agent reading all your emails and text messages and viewing your entire photo library? What if this same benevolent agent decided to install microphones and cameras in every single part of your house? Would you take issue with that? You're a well-behaved citizen. What could go wrong?



Me: If the government needs to do some surveillance to prevent a crime, that's acceptable.



Privacy Advocate: I agree. If the police, or military, need to target and observe a potential criminal or suspect, they can do that, with a temporary search warrant for a named individual. A judge should determine if there is probable cause and the authorities can move forward, once they have the proper permissions. Targeted and temporary surveillance, of potential suspects and criminals for an investigation, is a legal procedure. Perpetual mass surveillance, of non-criminals and non-suspects who have no part in the investigation, is not.







This “nothing to hide” argument is both archaic and unwise. Many big-government-lovers have used this line of thinking to justify mass surveillance, and other abuses of power

Keep in mind, if you extensively monitored any person, even a good person, you would be able to find singular pieces of information to smear, embarrass and even convict them of a crime

Think you're the exception? Think again.

There are countless laws on the books and, with some invasive personal surveillance and a little creativity, those compassionate government agents can take down anyone they consider to be an opponent of their party or agenda. Still think you're squeaky clean? Read Three Felonies A Day. This book explains how the average person, in the USA, unknowingly commits three felonies a day.





In the US, the government operates a program called PRISIM, in which they collect massive amounts of communications data on everyone they can.

When you use certain keywords (government agencies are hesitant to disclose such keywords), you can potentially become a flagged individual. Innocent yet curious search queries such as “Can you remove your own fingerprints?” or “Best places to stay in (insert enemy nation of the US)” could make you a prime candidate for tracking and detailed monitoring.

The harmless use of words like “bomb”, “terrorist attack” or “ISIS” could have similar consequences. Problematic messages could include “I bombed it. It was a hard test” or “I can't believe it's been 10 years since 9/11” or “Do you know anything about ISIS? Are they like a terror group?”.






Next time a government leader (such as a politician, police officer or IRS agent) uses this pitiful argument, invite them to provide 100% transparency of their digital data.

Demand of them what they are demanding of you. Ask if they are willing to unlock their smartphone and give it to you for an examination.

Their answer won't surprise you. Consider one adorable senator from California. She was enamored with massive government spying until she discovered the government was spying on her. Oh the irony.





Me: Ok I recognize that giving up my personal privacy is a bit of sacrifice, but I'm willing to do it. If I must trade my privacy for security, to prevent violent crimes, so be it. It's the moral and patriotic thing to do.



Privacy Advocate: Let me ask you this: Are you a violent criminal?



Me: No, of course not.



Privacy Advocate: Can you explain to me how the government collecting all digital data, of a non-criminal or non-suspect, is going to prevent a violent crime?



Me: Well not really. I'm not a legal or law enforcement expert.



Privacy Advocate: Ok well can you point me to a real example where mass surveillance, of innocent citizens, was an effective tool in preventing a violent act?

Me: No, again I'm not an expert on this stuff, but I trust those government security gurus are doing the right thing. I do recognize that some outspoken and controversial political activists could be at risk for privacy invasions, but I'm not a political activist. I'm not a public figure.


I know Martin Luther King Jr. was extensively wiretapped and harassed by the FBI. I also know that Jamal Khashoggi, a Saudi journalist, and critic of the government, was heavily tracked and was ultimately murdered by the government he criticized. The government was clearly wrong in these cases, but I'm not Martin Luther King Jr. or Jamal Khashoggi. I'm just an average citizen, minding my own business. Surely the government isn't seeking to invade the privacy of everyday people like me.



Privacy Advocate: Regrettably, governments, through mass surveillance, are violating the privacy of everyday people all the time. The details about such violations are rarely exposed because government agencies tend to avoid self-publicizing their dirty deeds.

Here are a few examples:

  1. Since 1997, city inspectors, in Rochester, New York, have violated the privacy of some renters by forcing them to allow the safety code inspectors to enter their residence. There was no probable cause, no warrant and no criminals in this case.

  2. According to an internal audit from 2013, the NSA is guilty of several thousand violations of privacy rules.

  3. For decades, the US, and other governments, have partnered to spy on each others' citizens. For example, while it might be illegal for the US government to wiretap one of their citizens, without a warrant, it is perfectly legal to have the UK wiretap this person and then hand the data over to the US government. You can read more about this lovely scheme here.

  4. According to a 2014 report, the NSA has secretly intercepted, recorded and archived almost every phone call in the Bahamas. I guess every person is the Bahamas must be a real suspect.

  5. An NSA program called XKEYSCORE, collects and processes Internet traffic from Americans, regardless of their criminality or non-criminality. It does the same for some foreign nationals. This data includes emails, chats and web-browsing traffic, pictures, documents, voice calls, webcam photos, web searches, social media traffic, logged keystrokes, intercepted username and password pairs, file uploads to online services and more. According to this 2015 article, the NSA puts more than 125,000 targets under surveillance each year. These people dont have to be suspected of any crime. They can be anyone who may have information bearing remotely on “foreign affairs.”

  6. The NSA also collects millions of email address books from around the world. At the time of this 2013 report, it had collected 44,743 e-mail address books from Yahoo, 105,068 from Hotmail, 82,857 from Facebook and 33,697 from Gmail.

  7. Without a warrant, prosecutors obtained 4 months of location history from cell phone records of Timothy Ivory Carpenter. He turned out to be a criminal, but they obtained this intimate information, without probable cause. What would stop them from getting the same data about you? If probable cause is not required then any person is fair game for this kind of aggressive snooping.

  8. In 2011 the government read and monitored the emails of Agron Hasbajrami, without a warrant. They accused him of having ties to a terror group. He did plead guilty to one of the charges, but this doesn't change the fact that read his emails, without a warrant, and they could do same to anyone of us


Me: Ok. I see that governments often violate the privacy of everyone they can. Honestly though, what''s the big deal? If anyone is going to invade my privacy, the government is probably the best entity to do so. We trust our elected officials to take care of the public interest and keep us safe and sometimes that means we give up some privacy to allow them to do their job.


Privacy Advocate: Well as we learned from the airport anecdote earlier, most people would not approve of unwarranted, unlimited and perpetual collection of their personal data. Violating the privacy of innocent people is morally wrong. Sadly, massive data collection can assist governments in executing much greater moral atrocities. This is not a trivial matter.

Consider just a few malicious government initiatives of the past:

  1. Japanese Internment Camps (WWII)
  2. The Holocaust (WWII)
  3. McCarthyism (Late 1940s)
  4. The Lavender Scare (Systematic discrimination and punishment of gays in 1953)
  5. The US military plotting a terrorist attack on American citizens, to justify war with Cuba (Operation Northwoods in 1962).
  6. The Genocide in Rwanda (1994)
  7. The Genocide in Cambodia (1970s)

Now consider how massive personal data collection could assist governments in planning and executing such evils. Data can be used as a weapon against any group, whether religious, ethnic, or political, that is deemed to be troublesome by the government. It is much easier to persecute, repress and silence a group if you know the personal details of its members. Data such as location history, political/religious affiliation, web browsing activity, phone calls, text messages, emails, personal habits. and personal health are very useful when fighting an opponent. Don't think you'll ever find yourself in a troublesome group? Read history and think again. Power shifts. Majorities can become minorities and vice versa. Massive data collection is a massive source of power. Such power often corrupts and, as a result, many good everyday people suffer.





Me: But we have internal rules in place to prevent this kind of abuse of privacy by the government? You know, checks and balances to keep the various agencies in line.



Privacy Advocate: There are some safeguards. Unfortunately, it is often difficult to “watch the watchers”. There are many cases in which spying agencies go unchecked or ignore their own rules.



Me: Well this got a little dark. I appreciate the points you've made about the government and I acknowledge the issues you’ve mentioned. There is a great deal of potential harm in massive data collection by governments.



Privacy Advocate Sorry, I don’t mean to frighten you. I’m not a doomsayer or pessimist by any measure. I just think we need to recognize the risks to our democracy and our way of life. Fear is counterproductive. We should focus on practical solutions to these issues. You can find some great solutions in the privacy guide.
