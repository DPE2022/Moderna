The "How" of Digital Privacy


Let me be clear. This guide is not going to make you invisible to government authorities who are trying to find you. It will not prevent you from serving a prison sentences for heinous crimes you've committed. This guide is here to help you opt out of mass surveillance. This kind of surveillance is the lazy and automated approach that collects data every day on everything from everyone, criminal and non-criminal alike. This guide is meant for those who want to no longer want to subject themselves to the standard abuses of their personal data.


First Word of Wisdom

Approach this your privacy journey with the positive attitude

Over the course of my “privacy journey”, I've had several key moments of realization. Each time I discovered an app or entity was collecting my data in an unsavory manner and using that data for an unrighteous purpose, I felt very upset and violated. This is a perfectly natural feeling, however, one should not be discouraged and think all is lost. I tried to channel my frustration into action by focusing on practical solutions to restore and protect my privacy.

I had been an avid user of Google, Facebook and other privacy violating products for 6+ years. I had allowed them to wrap their slimy-digital-data-sucking-tentacles around many personal elements of my life. I knew they had amassed a great deal of my very personal data and they had built a very robust, and revealing, profile of me. As I said, this did not make me feel good, but I refused to give up. There were steps I could take to have that data deleted, but my main goal was to ensure they could not collect anymore of my data.

Second Word of Wisdom

Approach your privacy journey with a practical and logical attitude

I am a big believer in practical solutions. If you wanted to be live an absolutely private life, I would suggest that you immediately close this webpage, cancel your internet service, throw away your smartphone and relocate to the desert of Western Australia. Of course, such a drastic lifestyle changes aren't very appealing to most people. The internet, and other modern technologies, provide so many incredible benefits. Let's not throw the baby out with the bath water. I simply hope I can provide with the tools to choose your own level privacy.

Each person should consider how they want to balance their personal privacy and personal convenience. Let me give a few examples to illustrate:

  • Flying On Airplanes:

    Since 9/11, flying privately or anonymously, on a commercial plane, in the US has become close to impossible. I would prefer to fly anonymously, if that were an option. I would prefer the airlines not collect hundreds of data points on my personal life and travel habits. However, flying across very long distances is so much better than driving. This is an instance in which I am willing to give up some privacy for the sake of convenience. I don't enjoy making this tradeoff, but I deem it necessary.

  • Streaming Music Online:

    The new business model of music streaming is very economical for average listeners like myself. If my music listening history were made public, I wouldn't blink an eye. I love the convenience and affordability of these services and I don't see a need to keep my music preference completely private.

  • Paying With Cash

    Your purchase history is probably one of your most revealing pieces of data. If I looked at your credit statement or amazon account, I could see where you shop, what you buy, what you window shop for, how often you buy, the quality of the products you like and many other telling details about you. If you wanted to purchase everything anonymously, using cash would be your best bet. However, while cash is quite private, it has its own set of disadvantages. Cash can stolen or lost. Cash can also make it very difficult to track your personal spending habits, if you're trying to stay on a budget. This is another area where you will have strike a balance between privacy and convenience.
